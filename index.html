Kurt Brendlinger

Post-doctoral Fellow at DESY Hamburg, with a focus on the Higgs, photons, and the ATLAS silicon tracker upgrade.

Current Projects

For more information on these subjects, see the public talks and publications.

ATLAS ITk

For this project, I work on an analytical thermo-electrical model of the ATLAS Inner Tracker, a silicon strip detector that is part of the Phase-II upgrade for the High-Luminosity LHC. The model predicts the thermal and electrical performance of the endcap system over its 14-year lifetime, accounting for radiation damage effects. The model takes input from FEA simulation, and allows for rapid testing of different system operational profiles and design choices. For more info, see this talk or our paper: arXiv:2003.00055.

H→γγ Measurements

The nature of the Higgs boson can now be probed with a moderate degree of precision using the ~155 fb−1 of collision data delivered by the LHC in Run 2. I focus on background modeling and related systematic uncertainties in measurements of Higgs couplings and differential cross sections, as well as searches for rare Higgs boson decays. For more info, see ATLAS-CONF-2018-028.

Photon Identification and Efficiency Measurements

Discriminating photons from jet (and particularly π0) backgrounds is a critical element of the ATLAS photon physics program, and precise efficiency measurements are just as important for precision cross section measurements. I work with members of the Photon Identification group on improving efficiency measurements and exploring machine learning techniques for improved identification discrimination. For more information, see arXiv:1908.00005.


Past Projects

ATLAS Electron Identification and Efficiency Measurements

I developed the current ATLAS electron identification using multivariate techniques (the Electron Likelihood) and made key improvements to efficiency measurements using Z→ee decays. The performance is documented here: arXiv:1612.01456

WZ Diboson Production Measurements

I worked on lepton background estimates for the first measurement of WZ diboson production at 13 TeV, using 3.2 fb−1 of data. The paper is here: arXiv:1606.04017.

H→ZZ*→4ℓ Measurements

I contributed to the first differential cross section measurements in this channel, and studied theory uncertainties, electron identification optimization and trigger strategies. The publication is here: arXiv:1408.5191

Transition Radiation Tracker Alignment

A well-aligned tracking detector is critical for the ATLAS physics program. I performed a straw-by-straw alignment of the ATLAS TRT using in-situ collision data, which identified large-scale detector deformations and imperfections in our detector description. For more information: ATLAS-CONF-2011-012.
